1. Whats the Goal?
#

As my first official post, I will write about what I believe is the ultimate way to achieve total anonymity on the web. To be clear, my intention is not to create an environment where we simply browse the web anonymously. The goal is to create another identity that can normally browse the web without barriers, but that cannot be linked back to you.

To achieve this, there are many methods, but the one I propose has no flaws from a privacy perspective (at most, it will be slow and painful). It takes into account a global adversary with access to worldwide network traffic and advanced AI for traffic recognition. Let’s start.

2. Use TailsOS
#

Even though what I’m about to describe is already extremely secure, the goal here is to prepare for the most ridiculous and worst-case scenarios. For the entire setup and future remote control of the VPS, I recommend using Tails OS from different geographical locations with public Wi-Fi (libraries, coffee shops, etc.).

The only situation where I see this being strictly necessary is if your VPN provider specifically targets your account—even though they would have no way of knowing you are connected to a VPS. I’m going to explain this in detail ahead.

3. Setup a Monero Wallet
#

Before I reveal the plan, we need a way to anonymously pay for services. Monero (XMR) is arguably the best digital currency on the planet. It is the gold standard for anonymity because its transactions are impossible to trace, offering the same privacy that physical cash does.

The plan begins by creating an account. I won’t go into every detail here, but you must buy your Monero on an offshore platform or a P2P market (like Haveno). While there are multiple ways to store crypto, the single most effective method is creating your own cold wallet. You could use a Raspberry Pi and open-source software to store your keys and validate transactions offline. I also recommend having more than one Monero wallet since we are going to purchase different services that shouldn’t be linked.

4. Setup Mullvad Vpn
#

Out of all current VPNs, Mullvad is widely recognized as the best for privacy. It requires no authentication or identity proof; you are just a random account number. You can pay with Monero, and the setup is instant.

While we can never be 100% sure of any provider, Mullvad has a proven track record. When their offices were raided in the past, the authorities found they actually had no data to expose. We use a VPN before the Tor network so your local ISP has no way to know your traffic is entering Tor. Furthermore, with advanced AI traffic detection, adding Mullvad makes the task of correlation significantly harder for a global adversary.

5. Renting a Ghost Vps
#

The next step is to rent a VPS. I recommend using a privacy-respecting company like Njalla or 1984 Hosting. Ensure you use a different Monero wallet for this than the one used for your VPN to prevent any financial cross-contamination.

Why use a VPS at all? Why not go straight to the web after the Tor network? There are two main reasons:

Reputation: Tor exit nodes have a bad reputation, which causes many websites to block you or put you on a watch list.

Metadata: Without a VPS, it is impossible to get rid of TCP/UDP packet metadata. If you use a standard VPN + Tor setup, the original packets from your local machine still reach the final destination. With a VPS, the packets are generated on the server. Since the VPS and your PC have different kernel metadata, you successfully decouple your hardware from your traffic.

6. Using the Tor Network
#

To safely connect your PC to the VPS, we will use the Tor network after the VPN. Instead of connecting to the VPS’s public IP address (which would let the VPS company see your VPN’s IP), we will set up an Onion Service on the VPS.

You will generate Tor keys on the VPS and configure a hidden service for SSH or remote access. This allows you to connect to your server using a .onion address. By doing this, the connection stays entirely within the Tor network. Even the VPS provider won’t know where the connection is coming from—to them, the server is just talking to itself via the Tor daemon.

7. Schizo Security: Remote Browser Control
#

As I mentioned, we aren’t just using the VPS as a simple proxy or Wireguard server. We will launch a browser inside the VPS and remote control the entire desktop. For this, we can use Kasm Workspaces.

Kasm uses a web-based protocol that is much better at handling the congestion and latency of the Tor network. Instead of sending raw network packets from your computer to a website, you are just receiving a stream of pixels. You can finally access the web with your second identity. Not even the NSA on steroids could trace this back to you, because even if they are spying on the VPS traffic, they only see the VPS interacting with the web—never your physical location.

8. Limitations
#

The main drawback is that it is extremely complex to set up for a beginner. Additionally, because we are layering a VPN, the Tor network, and a remote desktop protocol (which usually runs over TCP), the experience will be slow. The “input lag” can be painful, making this a setup for high-stakes privacy rather than daily entertainment.
